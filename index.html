FAQs

What should I wear?
Black tie optional. Bright colors, sparkles, and sequins highly encouraged.

Where should I stay?
Rates and availability are being impacted by a number of large events in Detroit during our wedding weekend, so you will want to shop around and book ASAP.

DoubleTree Suites by Hilton Hotel Detroit Downtown – Fort Shelby and Detroit Marriott at the Renaissance Center are good options in moderate walking distance. The Siren, Shinola, The Element, and Aloft are in very close walking distance but availability will be more limited. Hotel St. Regis is outside walking distance but is available at a lower rate and is convenient to public transit.

How should I get to the venue?
If you are staying at the Marriott, take the Detroit People Mover from the Renaissance Center stop (located on Tower 200, Level 2) to Broadway Street. You will arrive right at the entrance to the venue.

If you are staying at the DoubleTree, you can also take the People Mover from the Fort/Cass stop.

If you are driving, there will be complimentary valet parking at the venue. Navigate to 1435 Farmer Street and follow the signs for valet.

Regardless of your route, please be sure to leave plenty of time to arrive. There is a large event near the venue, so streets and transit may be more congested than usual.

ATTRACTIONS

Motown Museum

2648 Berry Gordy Jr. Boulevard
Detroit, MI 48208

Motown Museum is the ongoing heartbeat of the Motown legacy—a global tourist destination for music and history fans that celebrates the authentic story of Motown from its humble beginnings, to its emergence as the “Sound of Young America” and beyond. The Museum brings together fans, people and ideas from different generations and it captures the imagination to fuel dreams and inspire accomplishments.

ATTRACTIONS

Michigan Science Center

5020 John R Street
Detroit, MI 48202

The Michigan Science Center is a Smithsonian-affiliate science museum in Detroit, Michigan. The mission of the Michigan Science Center is a unique, fun and dynamic hands-on museum, offering programs that inspire curious minds of all ages to discover, explore and appreciate science, technology and math in a fun, dynamic learning environment. A great option if you are traveling with kids.

ATTRACTIONS

The Henry Ford

20900 Oakwood Blvd
Dearborn, MI 48124

Really pays homage to Detroit as the hub of American manufacturing. The Henry Ford is a 250 acre campus that includes: the Museum of American Innovation, Greenfield Village, and the Ford Rouge Factory. All of them are worth seeing if you have the time, though you will need to take a car to get there since it is located in neighboring Dearborn.

Museum: Henry Ford was a collector of all sorts of modern history artifacts, which you can find on display here, along with many exhibits across multiple facets of American industry.

Greenfield Village: Henry Ford literally collected famous historic sites and had them moved to Michigan to create this “village.” See the Wright Brothers’ original bike shop, Robert Frost’s house, and ride in a Model T.

Rouge Factory Tour: See modern Ford Motor Company manufacturing in real time.

ATTRACTIONS

Detroit Institute of Arts (DIA)

https://goo.gl/maps/NwAkNHXnaBjbpFdQ8
City

Right around the corner from the Michigan Science Center is the Detroit Institute of Arts, which houses one of the largest and most significant art collections in the US, and is one of the “Top 6 Museums in the US”. It’s a breathtaking space on the inside, featuring huge murals by Diego Rivera and an indoor courtyard. Houses multiple Van Gogh and Picasso pieces, along with countless other works. Plus, the building itself is an impressive work of architecture.

ATTRACTIONS

Guardian Building

500 Griswold Street
Detroit, MI 48226

The Guardian is currently an office building housing county government offices. Built in 1928 and finished in 1929, the building was originally called the Union Trust Building and is considered one of the boldest examples of art-deco architecture.

You can walk inside to see incredibly impressive architecture and design. The Fisher Building is another historic landmark and must-see for Detroit architecture.

VISIT

Corktown


Corktown is a trendy, youthful area with industrial-chic bars, intimate live-music venues, and hip breweries. Slows Bar BQ is a favored food joint, but there are also relaxed spots to eat Mexican and Japanese cuisine. Thrift stores, quirky boutiques, and secondhand record stores line the streets. The vast, derelict Beaux Arts building housing the Michigan Central Train Depot is earmarked for development.

VISIT

Mexicantown


Mexicantown-Southwest Detroit is a down-to-earth neighborhood filled with informal restaurants offering traditional Mexican food, from tamales to tacos. Laid-back bars and tiki lounges serve margaritas; grocery stores brim with corn and plantains; and old-school bakeries sell sugary churros. Family-oriented Clark Park is a popular spot for baseball, while cultural center El Museo del Norte showcases Latino art.

VISIT

Boston-Edison Historic District


The Boston-Edison area was designed and developed by some of Detroit’s most prominent turn-of-the century citizens. Edward Voigt foresaw the logical growth of Detroit along Woodward Avenue and began acquiring land as early as 1884, platting the area between Woodward and Hamiliton (Voigt Park Subdivision) in 1891.

There is incredibly impressive architecture and landscaping in this area. It is mostly residential; the homes themselves are the primary attraction.

VISIT

Midtown


Midtown is just north of the downtown area where the wedding is located, and it is home to many of the main attractions in Detroit. Take the QLine to get there quickly and easily.

A couple of miles north of Downtown on Woodward Avenue (and linked by the efficient QLine Streetcar), Detroit’s Midtown neighborhood is the city’s cultural hub. All the big museums are here; the world class Detroit Institute of Arts (famed for its giant Diego Rivera mural), the Museum of Contemporary Art, Charles H. Wright Museum of African American History, the Detroit Historical Museum, and Michigan Science Center. The district encompasses the campus of Wayne State University, and contains numerous art galleries, quirky stores and excellent restaurants (The Whitney is one of the city’s best). Adjacent New Center, just north of I-94, is home to the iconic Motown Museum and the original Ford Piquette Avenue Plant.

VISIT

Ferndale


Ferndale is a separate city, located about 15 minutes from downtown.

Ferndale is a destination known for art, music and DIY culture, and it is well known in the Detroit area for its LGBT population and progressive policies. It offers independent shops, support for local makers, and has a diverse and vibrant food scene. There are great restaurants, bars, and shopping, and it is well worth a visit if you are in town for a few days.
